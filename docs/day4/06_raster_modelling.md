# Raster-Based Hydrological and Soil Erosion Modelling

Raster grids allow us to model complex environmental processes (such as surface runoff generation and soil erosion rates) by combining multiple spatial variables. Each cell acts as a local calculation node, enabling catchment-wide modeling. This section covers runoff modeling (Rational and SCS Curve Number methods) and soil erosion susceptibility modeling using the RUSLE equation.

---

## 1. Surface Runoff Modeling

Estimating peak runoff volume is critical for designing drainage infrastructure, culverts, and flood mitigation structures.

### The Rational Method (Peak Discharge)

The Rational Method is a simple empirical model used to calculate peak surface runoff discharge at a catchment outlet:

$$Q = C \times I \times A$$

Where:
* $Q$ is the peak runoff rate (in cubic meters per second, $m^3/\text{sec}$).
* $C$ is the dimensionless runoff coefficient, representing the fraction of rainfall that becomes runoff (e.g., $0.90$ for paved areas, $0.15$ for dense forests). In QGIS, this is generated by reclassifying vector land cover and soil type layers.
* $I$ is the rainfall intensity (in millimeters per hour, $mm/\text{hr}$), derived from IDF (Intensity-Duration-Frequency) curves for a design storm.
* $A$ is the catchment drainage area (in square meters, $m^2$), calculated directly from flow accumulation boundaries.

### The SCS Curve Number (CN) Method

Developed by the USDA Soil Conservation Service, this model estimates total runoff volume from a storm event based on land cover, soil hydrologic properties, and antecedent soil moisture.

* **Runoff Equation:**
  $$Q = \frac{(P - I_a)^2}{(P - I_a) + S}$$
  Where:
  * $Q$ is the cumulative runoff depth ($mm$).
  * $P$ is the cumulative rainfall depth ($mm$).
  * $I_a$ is the initial abstraction (water intercepted by vegetation or depressions, typically assumed to be $0.2 \times S$).
  * $S$ is the maximum potential soil water retention ($mm$), calculated from the Curve Number ($CN$):
    $$S = \frac{25400}{CN} - 254$$

* **Hydrologic Soil Groups (HSG):** Soils are classified into four groups based on infiltration rates:
  * *Group A:* Sand/Gravel. High infiltration, low runoff potential.
  * *Group B:* Sandy loam. Moderate infiltration.
  * *Group C:* Clayey loam. Low infiltration.
  * *Group D:* Clay. Very low infiltration, high runoff potential.

* **GIS Implementation:** You combine soil group polygons and landuse rasters in QGIS using a matrix join to assign a Curve Number ($CN$ value, from $30$ to $100$) to every grid cell. The resulting $CN$ raster is used in the Raster Calculator to model spatial runoff potential.

## 2. Soil Erosion Susceptibility (The RUSLE Model)

The **Revised Universal Soil Loss Equation (RUSLE)** calculates average annual soil loss caused by sheet and rill erosion:

$$A = R \times K \times LS \times C \times P$$

Where:
* $A$ is the estimated average annual soil loss (in tons per hectare per year, $\text{t/ha/yr}$).
* $R$ is the rainfall-runtoff erosivity factor, representing the kinetic energy of rainfall (derived from interpolated station grids).
* $K$ is the soil erodibility factor, representing soil susceptibility to detachment (derived from soil map textures).
* $LS$ is the topographic factor representing slope length ($L$) and slope steepness ($S$), calculated directly from a DEM.
* $C$ is the cover management factor, representing the vegetation canopy cover effect (often derived from NDVI satellite grids).
* $P$ is the conservation support practice factor (e.g., terracing, contour farming).

---

## 3. Calculating the LS Factor in QGIS/SAGA

In complex terrain (such as the steep slopes of Nepal), calculating the $LS$ factor manually is impossible. We utilize SAGA's specialized terrain modules:

1. Open the **Processing Toolbox** and navigate to **SAGA** > **Terrain Analysis - Hydrology** > **LS Factor**.

2. **Elevation:** Select your conditioned, filled DEM.

3. **Slope:** Select your calculated slope raster.

4. **Area:** Select your flow accumulation raster.

5. **Method:** Select **Moore et al. (1991)** (the standard algorithm for modeling multi-directional overland flow).

6. Click **Run**. The output is an $LS$ factor raster grid.

### Compiling the Final Soil Erosion Grid

Once all five factor rasters ($R, K, LS, C, P$) are prepared at the same resolution and spatial alignment, compile them using the **Raster Calculator**:

`"R_factor@1" * "K_factor@1" * "LS_factor@1" * "C_factor@1" * "P_factor@1"`

The resulting raster grid maps annual soil erosion rates, allowing WECS planners to locate watershed zones requiring immediate soil conservation or terracing interventions.
